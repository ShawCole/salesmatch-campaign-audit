The Story

What the data actually shows

Core Finding
The two phases are not comparable. At least six variables changed between them — including creative format, ad set structure, per-video budget, and runtime. The custom audience was only one of them.

Phase 1's intent-audience campaigns ran in video-only configuration for just 1-2 days before being killed on April 19. Phase 2's cold-audience campaigns have been running video-only for 4+ days. Meta's learning phase requires 5-7 days minimum to stabilize on a conversion objective. One test was run to completion. The other was killed before learning could start.

The Hidden Positive Signal

Inside Phase 1, on the intent audience Craig's report calls "low quality with no real intent," one video achieved a 32-second average play time (CSM Script 1). That is the single strongest engagement signal anywhere in either phase — and it was produced against the audience the report blames. Users on a "low-quality" audience do not watch videos for half a minute. They scroll in 2-3 seconds.

What Actually Changed Between Phase 1 and Phase 2

  1. Audience — Intent custom audience → cold broad targeting
  2. Creative mix — Video + display → video only
  3. Ad set count — 7 ad sets → 3 ad sets
  4. Creatives per ad set — 6-8 → 3-4
  5. Per-video budget — ~$28 avg → ~$57 avg (2x)
  6. Runtime — Killed at day 5 → still running at day 4+

The report attributes 100% of the performance difference to variable #1. No controlled test was run to isolate that variable from the other five.

Section 05

The Comparison the Report Made vs. The Actual Comparison

The audit report concluded "signal failure on the custom audience" by comparing Phase 1 (intent) to Phase 2 (cold). But the two phases differ on six structural variables, not just audience. The only honest conclusion from the data we have: we don't yet know.

Phase 1 — Intent Audience Test

Apr 15-19 · $1,623 spent · 0 leads
  • 7 ad sets with $30-100/day budgets (fragmented)
  • 6-8 creatives per ad set (video + display mixed)
  • ~50% of budget consumed by display ads
  • Displays shut off around Apr 17 by CMO
  • Video-only configuration for ~1.5 days
  • Killed Apr 19 — before learning phase completion
  • Per-video average budget: ~$28

Phase 2 — Cold Audience Test

Apr 19-22+ · $625 spent · 3 leads ($208 CPR)
  • 3 ad sets consolidated with larger per-set budget
  • 3-4 creatives per ad set (video only)
  • 0% of budget on displays
  • No display ads existed at launch
  • Video-only configuration for 4+ days
  • Still running — approaching learning phase stability
  • Per-video average budget: ~$57 (2x Phase 1)

The Six Changed Variables

To attribute Phase 2's improvement solely to the audience change, all other variables would need to be held constant. They were not. Between Phase 1 and Phase 2:

  1. Audience changed (intent → cold)
  2. Creative format changed (video + display → video only)
  3. Ad set count changed (7 → 3)
  4. Creatives per ad set changed (6-8 → 3-4)
  5. Per-video budget changed (~$28 → ~$57)
  6. Runtime changed (killed at day 5 → 4+ days and running)

Any one of variables 2-6 could fully explain Phase 2's results without variable 1 being a factor at all. The display reallocation alone (freeing up ~50% of budget for video) combined with consolidated ad-set structure is a common cause of lift in B2B campaigns, independent of audience.

That ad was running in AdSet B and was not given time to convert — the campaign was killed on day 5, before learning phase could complete.

Section 07

What the Report Does Not Address

Several technical variables that strongly affect Meta campaign performance are not mentioned in the audit report. Any one of these could explain the Phase 1 results without any audience quality issue.

◆ Meta Employment Special Ad Category

  • Ads promoting staffing or recruitment services are often flagged under Meta's Employment Special Ad Category
  • Flagged campaigns have severe custom-audience delivery restrictions as of March 2025
  • CPMs of $276-$524 observed in Phase 1 are a textbook signature of Employment category throttling, not low audience quality
  • Low-quality audiences produce cheap clicks. Throttled campaigns produce expensive nothing. Phase 1 matches the second pattern.
  • The report makes no mention of verifying Special Ad Category status

◆ Learning Phase Math

  • Meta's learning phase requires ~50 conversion events per ad set per week to exit
  • Phase 1's 7 ad sets at $30-100/day budgets could not reach this threshold in 5 days
  • None of the Phase 1 ad sets exited learning before being killed
  • During learning, Meta does not optimize delivery — CPMs run higher and delivery is inefficient regardless of audience
  • The report attributes learning-phase behavior to audience quality

◆ Display Ad Contamination

  • ~50% of Phase 1 budget went to display ad units that produced zero leads
  • B2B service advertising on Meta display placements consistently underperforms feed/Reels placements
  • Display spend in the same ad sets as video creative pulls budget away from video optimization
  • The report does not isolate video-only spend from display spend in its analysis
  • Removing displays alone (without changing audience) has been documented to produce lift in comparable B2B campaigns

◆ Creative Hold Rate Analysis

  • Industry benchmark for B2B video hold rate: 15%+
  • Most videos in both phases held at 2-7%
  • Hook rates landed (20-39%) but videos failed to retain attention past the hook
  • This pattern indicates a creative problem, not an audience problem
  • The report states "this is not a creative issue" without addressing hold rate data

◆ Instant Form Quality Trade-Off

  • Meta Instant Forms produce 30-50% cheaper leads but significantly lower quality vs landing pages
  • B2B booked-call funnels convert poorly from Instant Forms to actual sales conversations
  • The report cites Instant Forms as proof that "no funnel friction" exists
  • But the low quality of Instant Form leads in B2B can mask the impact of a functioning audience
  • A landing page with Calendly would be the appropriate test vehicle for this offer

◆ Frequency Under-Delivery

  • B2B conversion campaigns typically require 3-5 touches per user to convert
  • Phase 1 frequency: 1.0-1.7 — below the threshold for B2B conversion
  • The report cites this as proof of "no fatigue" — but it also indicates no retargeting depth
  • Users on the intent audience weren't hit enough times to convert, regardless of intent quality
  • This is a budget/reach problem, not an audience problem
Section 08

The Proper Next Step

Before concluding anything about the audience, run a controlled test that isolates the audience variable. Hold everything else constant.

Proposed Controlled Split Test

Two ad sets, identical in every way except audience:

  • Same winning video — CSM Script 1 (the 32s hold video) or the Phase 2 winner (B2B Script 3 with 2 leads)
  • Same budget — $100-150/day per ad set
  • Same lead form — OR switch both to Calendly landing page for quality
  • Same campaign objective — Leads / Conversions
  • Minimum 14-day runtime — non-negotiable, past learning phase with margin
  • Pre-launch audit of Special Ad Category status — confirm not flagged as Employment

What the Existing Data Does and Doesn't Support

The data DOES support:

  • Display ads contaminated Phase 1's budget — ~50% spent on units that produced zero leads
  • The 32-second hold time on the intent audience indicates real engagement
  • Phase 2's structural improvements (video-only, consolidated ad sets, longer runtime) are sufficient to explain its $208/result performance
  • Phase 1 was killed before learning phase completed

The data DOES NOT support:

  • A conclusion that the intent audience signal is broken
  • A conclusion that the audience needs to be replaced
  • A conclusion that "this is not a creative issue" (hold rates contradict)
  • A comparison between the two phases on audience alone (six variables changed)

Bottom Line

The audit report's conclusion — Signal Quality Failure — is not supported by the data presented. It is a plausible hypothesis, but it is untested. The only way to test it is with a controlled experiment that holds five of the six variables constant and varies only the audience. That test has not been run.

Until that test is run, the correct conclusion is: "We don't yet know whether the intent audience works or not, because the Phase 1 test was structured in a way that made it impossible to isolate."
